## Motivation

Several notations are in common use to pick out morphisms in categorical
structures. Among text-based notations, the most important distinction is
between **point-free** notations, which do not involve "elements" or
"variables," and **pointful** notations, which do. For example, suppose $f: X
\to Y$ and $g: Y \to Z$ are composable morphisms in a category. Their composite
can be denoted in point-free notation as $g \circ f$ or $f \cdot g$ and in
pointful notation as `|x: X| g(f(x))` (a Rust-style programming syntax) or $x: X
\vdash g(f(x)): Z$ (a type-theoretic syntax).

In a bare category, the most general form of composition is of a path of
morphisms, and there is little practical difference between the pointful and
point-free notations. However, as more categorical structure is introduced, the
difference can become dramatic. As anothr example---still small by practical
standards---suppose $f: X \times Y \to W$, $g: X \to W'$, and $h: W \times W'
\to Z$ are morphisms in a cartesian category. The composite morphism with
signature $X \times Y \to Z$ is written in point-free notation as

$$
(\Delta_X \times 1_Y) \cdot (1_X \times \sigma_{X, Y}) \cdot (f \times g) \cdot h,
$$

which the reader must exert some effort to compile in their head, in contrast to
the pointful notation

$$
x : X,\ y : Y \vdash h(f(x, y), g(x)) : Z, 
$$

which is just the familiar syntax for composing functions. Besides familiarity,
the key ergonomic advantage of pointful syntax is that it operations like
copying and permuting that must be explicitly stated in point-free syntax become
*implicit* in pointful syntax.

A less obvious use of pointful notation is as a tool to find *normal forms* for
morphisms in free categorical structures. Consider again morphisms in a bare
category. Under the usual biased axiomatization of a category, a ternary
composite of morphisms $f$, $g$, and $h$ admits two different expressions, $h
\circ (g \circ f)$ and $(h \circ g) \circ f$, denoting the same morphism, by the
associativity law. Yet, using pointful notation, it is possible to form only
*one* term denoting this morphism, namely $x : X \vdash h(g(f(x))): Z$. Thus the
pointful notation gives a normal form for morphisms in a free category. Of
course, in this case, it is just as easy to produce a normal form without using
variables. That's exactly what the existing
[`Path`](https://next.catcolab.org/dev/rust/catlog/one/path/enum.path) data
structure in `catlog` does.

Finding normal forms becomes more difficult as categorical structure
accumulates. For example, let $X \xto{f} Y \xto{g} Z$ and $X' \xto{f'} Y'
\xto{g'} Z'$ be morphisms in a monoidal category. In the textual, point-free
syntax, two different expressions, $(g \circ f) \otimes (g' \circ f')$ and $(g
\otimes g') \circ (f \otimes f')$, denote the same morphism, by the interchange
law. Moreover, due to the symmetry of the situation, there is no obvious reason
to prefer one over the other.[^string-diagrams] In the point-free notation of a
suitable type theory, there will be a canonical way to denote this morphism,
namely

$$
(x, x'): X \otimes X' \vdash (g(f(x)), g'(f'(x'))): Z \otimes Z'.
$$

Intuitively, this term corresponds to the first of the above expressions,
whereas the second cannot be constructed because applying the tensor product $g
\otimes g'$ is not allowed.

[^string-diagrams]:
  Among category theorists, the usual recipe to get normal forms for morphisms
  in (symmetric) monoidal categories is to use *string diagrams* or, what is
  closely related, *wiring diagrams* [@patterson-et-al-2020]. While such
  diagrams should be a part of CatColab, this document is concerned solely with
  textual syntax.
