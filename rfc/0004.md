## Motivation

Several notations are in common use to denote morphisms in categorical
structures. Among text-based notations, the most important distinction is
between **point-free** notations, which do not involve "elements" or
"variables," and **pointful** notations, which do. Suppose, for example, that
$f: X \to Y$ and $g: Y \to Z$ are composable morphisms in a category. Their
composite can be denoted in point-free notation as $g \circ f$ or $f \cdot g$
and in pointful notation as `|x: X| g(f(x))` (to use a Rust-style programming
syntax) or $x: X \vdash g(f(x)): Z$ (to use a type-theoretic syntax).

In a bare category, the most general composite is of a path of morphisms, and
there is little practical difference between the pointful and point-free
notations. However, as more categorical structure is introduced, the difference
can become dramatic. As another example---still small by practical
standards---suppose that $f: X \times Y \to W$, $g: X \to W'$, and $h: W \times
W' \to Z$ are morphisms in a cartesian category. The composite morphism with
signature $X \times Y \to Z$ is written in point-free notation as

$$
(\Delta_X \times 1_Y) \cdot (1_X \times \sigma_{X, Y}) \cdot (f \times g) \cdot h,
$$

which the reader must exert some effort to compile in their head, in contrast to
the pointful notation

$$
x : X,\ y : Y \vdash h(f(x, y), g(x)) : Z, 
$$

which is just the familiar syntax for composing functions. Besides its
familiarity, the key ergonomic advantage of pointful syntax is that it
operations like copying and permuting that must be explicitly stated in
point-free syntax become *implicit* in pointful syntax.

A less obvious use of pointful notation is finding [canonical
forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_form) for morphisms in free
categorical structures. Consider again morphisms in a bare category. With
point-free syntax following the standard biased axioms for a category, a ternary
composite of morphisms $f$, $g$, and $h$ admits two different expressions, $h
\circ (g \circ f)$ and $(h \circ g) \circ f$, that denote the same morphism, by
the associativity law. Yet, using pointful notation, it is possible to form only
*one* term denoting this morphism, namely $x : X \vdash h(g(f(x))): Z$. The
pointful notation thus gives a canonical form for morphisms in a free category.
Of course, in this case, it is just as easy to produce a canonical form without
using variables. That's exactly what the standard
[`Path`](https://next.catcolab.org/dev/rust/catlog/one/path/enum.path) data
structure in `catlog` does.

Finding canonical forms becomes more difficult as categorical structure
accumulates. For example, let $X \xto{f} Y \xto{g} Z$ and $X' \xto{f'} Y'
\xto{g'} Z'$ be morphisms in a monoidal category. In point-free syntax, two
different expressions,

$$
(g \circ f) \otimes (g' \circ f')
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
(g \otimes g') \circ (f \otimes f'),
$$

denote the same morphism, by the interchange law. Moreover, due to the symmetry
of the situation, there is no obvious reason to prefer one over the other. In
the point-free notation of a suitable type theory, this symmetry is broken, and
there will be a canonical way to denote this morphism, namely

$$
(x, x'): X \otimes X' \vdash (g(f(x)), g'(f'(x'))): Z \otimes Z'.
$$

Intuitively, this term corresponds to the first of the above point-free
expressions, whereas the second has no counterpart because applying the tensor
product $g \otimes g'$ is not allowed. This point of view---that type theory is
a toolkit to find canonical forms for morphisms---is a theme in Shulman's
lecture notes on categorical logic [@shulman-2016].

In short, among textual notations, pointful notation has both practical
advantages, being familiar and ergonomic, as well as theoretical ones, inasmuch
as it sometimes leads to canonical forms.

Before moving on, though, it's worth acknowledging that point-free notation is
not without its own merits. Conveniently for computer implementation, it can be
mechanically extracted for categorical structures defined in any reasonable
formal system, such as GATs or double theories. In contrast, while type theory
as a field has recurring principles and techniques, designing *a* type theory
remains a bespoke and fiddly business. Indeed, the research problem behind this
RFC is that, at the level of *semantics*, CatColab has a unifying meta-logic for
(the lower hierarchy of) categorial logic, and we now seek a unifying approach
for the corresponding type-theoretic *syntax*. How far this can be pushed
without resorting to ad hoc tricks remains to be seen. Relatedly, point-free
syntax is readily available for *any* categorical structure and is equivariant
under categorical duality, whereas pointful notation is intrinsically biased
toward the domain of a morphism (its inputs) and, to a lesser extent, toward
cartesian products. The happiest and most classical categorical setting for
pointful notation is a free cartesian multicategory or, what is essentially the
same thing, a free cartesian category whose basic morphisms have codomains that
are basic objects. The further one departs from this setting, the more likely
standard techniques are to break down. Nevertheless, a pointful textual syntax
seems so indispensable to realizing the vision of CatColab as a family of
interoperable programming languages that pursuing a unified approach to syntax
cannot be neglected.

## Rationale and alternatives

### String diagrams

Type theory is one methodology to find canonical forms for morphisms, but it's
not the only one. Among applied category theorists, the usual recipe to produce
canonical forms for morphisms in monoidal categories is to use *string diagrams*
or their combinatorial counterparts, *wiring diagrams* [@patterson-et-al-2020].
Incidentally, string diagrams are a point-free syntax, unlike type theory.

From our "semantics first" perspective, string diagrams are not so much an
alternative to type-theoretic textual notation as a complement to it. In
CatColab, it is the categorical structures that are primary; both graphical and
textual syntaxes should be available. Making multiple syntaxes work together
seamlessly poses architectural challenges. This document, however, is solely
about textual syntax.
